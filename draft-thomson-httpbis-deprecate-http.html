Deprecating the 'http:' URI Scheme
draft-thomson-httpbis-deprecate-http-latest

Abstract

The http: URI scheme has proven to be problematic. This document deprecates its use.

1. Introduction

Anyone who uses HTTP [RFC2616] without TLS [RFC5246] (i.e., HTTPS [RFC2818]) runs a very real risk of being pwned.

This document attempts to remedy this by prohibiting the use of the http: scheme.

1.1. Conventions and Terminology

In cases where normative language needs to be emphasized, this document falls back on established shorthands for expressing interoperability requirements on implementations: the capitalized words "MUST", "MUST NOT", "SHOULD" and "MAY". The meaning of these is described in [RFC2119].

2. MUST NOT use http:

HTTP clients and servers MUST NOT access or provide resources over unsecured HTTP. Thus, resources identified with http: URIs cannot be used.

3. Why Not Opportunistically Encrypt?

Opportunistic encryption is fine if your adversary isn't on-path. It's pretty ineffectual if your adversary is able to interspose themselves somehow. There is now evidence to suggest that interception capabilities are less difficult to achieve than originally thought.

4. Why Not Trust on First Use?

TOFU is unsatisfactory. It is effectively a pin for the lifetime of a certificate, which raises several issues:

  • Key rollover cannot occur without exposure to an active attack, or some form of succession plan. The typical key pinning succession plan involves providing alternative pins under the protection of a trusted certificate. This sort of plan cannot be enacted without a lead time of at least equal to the pin duration. The successor key has to be available for this entire period.

    From an operational perspective, this is difficult. In order to ensure that the risk of loss, exposure, or exfiltration of the private key for the successor, the private key for the successor needs to be backed up and stored offline. Any service that is unwilling to obtain third-party validation of their certificates seems unlikely to be inclined to perform this step.
  • Pinning makes it difficult to scale out services without sharing of private keys. Clients will reject connections that offer private keys other than the one they first see.

5. Why Not Externalize the Consequences?

That's what we've been doing for years. That turned out to be a political nightmare once people found out.

6. Security Considerations

The IETF has partnered with several large organizations to perform enforcement. Users of http: URIs will be pwned. Operators of sites that offer http: resources will be pwned.
